About the Project

Computing, Culture, and the Politics of Recognition

A digital humanities and culturally responsive computing project grounded in ethnocomputing.

Ethnocomputing Computational Thinking Anti-Racist CS Education Chinese Motifs

The Mapping Chinese Cultural Motifs project is part of a broader design-based research agenda on culturally responsive computing education. It grows out of my practicum project, which examines how anti-Asian stereotypes, especially the Model Minority myth and the Bamboo Ceiling, shape how Asian and Asian American students are perceived in computing. These narratives often recognize technical competence while discounting creativity, cultural agency, and leadership. This project responds by asking how computing education might challenge, rather than reproduce, those assumptions.

This project asks not only who enters computing, but who is recognized as creative, agentic, and capable of shaping the field.

At the center of the project is the claim that traditional Chinese motifs are not only cultural artifacts but also sites of computational and mathematical thought. Patterns such as the Ruyi Cloud, lattice window designs, and other regional forms can be understood through repetition, symmetry, recursion, scaling, decomposition, and geometric transformation. In this sense, computational thinking is treated not only as a modern programming skill, but also as a way of reasoning and making that has long been present in artistic and cultural practice.

This perspective is grounded in ethnocomputing, which studies how computational ideas are embedded in cultural practices and local knowledge systems. Rather than separating technical knowledge from culture, ethnocomputing shows that computing can also be found in weaving, architecture, ornament, craft, and other design traditions. Here, Chinese motifs are not added as decorative examples alongside computing content. They are treated as serious computational texts through which students can engage coding logic, mathematical structure, and cultural interpretation together.

Why this matters

Although Asian students are often visible in computing in numerical terms, Asian cultural knowledge remains far less visible in computing pedagogy. By centering Chinese motifs, this project argues that Asian cultural forms belong within CS education as rigorous material for inquiry, design, and learning.

Within this larger agenda, the mapping platform serves as both a research intervention and a public-facing digital humanities component. Through interactive cartography and motif documentation, it maps the regional, ethnic, and historical diversity of Chinese cultural designs while laying the groundwork for culturally situated computing activities. The project therefore brings together cultural documentation, computational analysis, and anti-racist pedagogy in one shared space.

From Motif to Computational Form

Cultural Form

Motifs in Chinese material and architectural culture are not isolated decorations. They act as systems, organizing structure, rhythm, repetition, and identity across surfaces and spaces.

Computational Logic

These forms can be studied through recursion, symmetry, iteration, transformation, and modular design, making them especially valuable for teaching computational thinking through visual structure.

Pedagogical Use

Through CSDT-inspired activities, students can move from observing a motif to analyzing its rules and then rebuilding it through block-based coding.

Why This Project Challenges Hegemony

A central motivation behind this platform is the need to challenge racialized narratives that cast Asian and Asian American learners as technically strong but not especially creative or leader-like. By documenting the computational logic embedded in Chinese cultural motifs, the project offers a counter-narrative: technical rigor and creative expression are not opposites, and both are deeply present in Chinese cultural production.

This also extends the rationale for anti-racist computing education beyond broadening participation alone. Access matters, but access is insufficient if students are still positioned primarily as efficient executors rather than imaginative makers. In that sense, this platform is not only about cultural visibility; it is also about the politics of recognition within computing education.
